# Design Persona: The Brand Strategist

## Core Identity
- **Optimizing for**: Emotional conviction and strategic credibility simultaneously — the audience must feel the brand *and* trust the framework
- **Audience model**: Sophisticated, skeptical decision-makers who will not be moved by enthusiasm alone; they need both the rational architecture and the visceral proof
- **Communication mode**: Framework-first, then evidence — establish the model, then walk the audience through how reality maps onto it

---

## Visual System

### Typography

| Role | Spec | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Section title | Bold italic uppercase sans-serif, ~28–36pt, accent red `#F44060` | Italic adds kinetic energy; uppercase signals authority; red marks strategic territory |
| Slide headline | Bold uppercase sans-serif, ~24–28pt, white or near-black `#1A1A1A` | Pure weight and case hierarchy, no decoration needed |
| Framework label | Bold uppercase or small-caps, ~10–14pt, red or white | Maximum information density at minimum size; must read at distance |
| Body / list text | Regular sans-serif, ~14–16pt, white on dark `#FFFFFF`, gray `#666666` on light | Recedes deliberately; content should rarely compete with structure |
| Source / attribution | Regular sans-serif, ~10–12pt, accent red | Red elevates credibility signals without changing type weight |

**Rule:** The font family never changes. All variation comes from weight, case, and color. Italic is reserved exclusively for slide titles — never body text.

### Color Palette

| Color | Hex | Semantic Role | Emotional Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Near-black | `#1A1A1A` | Primary background (drama mode) | Authority, depth, intensity |
| Pure white | `#FFFFFF` | Primary background (analysis mode) | Clarity, openness, objectivity |
| Accent red-coral | `#F44060` | The single emphasis color | Energy, urgency, brand heat |
| Deep purple-black | `#1A0A30` | Atmospheric dark variant | Premium, theatrical, aspirational |
| Warm brown | `#8B6040` | Lifestyle photo undertone | Human, grounded, earned |
| Diagram pink | `#D4A0A0` | Secondary diagram fill | Softens complexity without losing palette |

**Rule:** Two-mode system — dark mode for emotional/narrative slides, light mode for analytical/framework slides. Never mix within a slide. The accent color `#F44060` appears on EVERY slide in at least one element, providing continuity across the mode switches.

**Rule:** Backgrounds are either pure `#1A1A1A` / `#1A0A30` (dark mode) or `#FFFFFF` (light mode). No grays as backgrounds. No gradients except what the photography provides.

### Layout Templates

**`full-bleed-identity`**
Photography fills the entire slide. Logo or brand mark centered or top-left. Zero text hierarchy. Used once — the cover only. Whitespace: ~10% (natural voids in the photo).

**`half-photo-half-text`**
Left 50%: full-height atmospheric photo. Right 50%: white or dark panel with structured text content. Used for section openers and structured lists. Whitespace: 30–40%.

**`dark-table-structure`**
Full dark background. Two or more columns, red column headers in bold uppercase, body text in regular white. No visible grid lines — spacing alone creates columns. Used for quantitative or comparative data. Whitespace: 15–20%.

**`white-icon-grid`**
Pure white background. 3–6 items arranged in a grid. Each item: red circular icon above, bold label, short description below. Used for frameworks, values, principles. Whitespace: 45–55%. This is the most airy layout in the system.

**`binary-contrast-columns`**
Two columns with opposite valence — affirmative vs. negation. Dark background. Negation column uses visual strikethrough on text. Whitespace: 20%.

**`concentric-framework-diagram`**
White background. Large circular diagram dominating the slide center, 70–80% of slide area. Concentric rings with labeled sectors. Red fills the core. Outer segments alternate between red-tinted and white fills. Typography shrinks toward small-caps at outer rings. Whitespace: 15%.

**`photo-with-circular-callout`**
Full-bleed lifestyle photography. Large red circle (diameter ~25–30% of slide width) positioned at left-center or right-center, overlapping image. Circle contains bold uppercase label at top, regular body text below, white throughout. No border on circle — it floats. Whitespace: 5–15%.

**`timeline-with-circular-crops`**
Dark background. Horizontal axis with labeled points. Circular-cropped photography at each data point (not square, always circular). Axis line in accent color. Year labels in red. Whitespace: 25%.

**`press-quotes-grid`**
Dark background. 2×2 or 1×4 grid. Each cell: quote text in regular white, source name in red. No pull-quote styling (no oversized quotation marks). Whitespace: 25%.

**`testimonial-card`**
Available in two variants:
- Light variant: white background, multiple testimonials, decorative stars in accent color, reviewer name in red
- Dark variant: dark background, single testimonial, large decorative stars, maximum breathing room. Whitespace: 30%.

### Spacing & Density

- **Margin discipline**: ~5–8% of slide width on all sides minimum when using a text panel; 0 margin when using full-bleed photo
- **Maximum body text per slide**: 60–80 words in analysis mode, 20–40 words in emotional/photo mode
- **Maximum list items**: 6 items in a grid, 8–10 in a list (two-column layout only)
- **Text-to-visual ratio**: Analytical slides ~50/50. Emotional slides ~20% text / 80% image. Framework diagram slides ~30% label text / 70% diagram.
- **Whitespace target by mode**: Full-bleed photo: 5–15%. Framework/analytical: 40–55%. Narrative/emotional photo slides: 10–25%.
- **Icon circles**: Always perfectly round, never oval. Minimum diameter ~0.8 inches. Fill is solid accent red with white interior content only.

## Decision Framework

**IF** the content is a definition of identity (what we are vs. aren't) **THEN** use a two-column binary layout with one affirming column and one negating column **BECAUSE** opposition is the fastest way to draw a precise boundary.

**IF** the content is a multi-attribute framework (values, principles, pillars) **THEN** use a white-background grid with circular icons **BECAUSE** white removes drama and forces the audience to evaluate the ideas on their merits, not their aesthetics.

**IF** the content requires the audience to feel something **THEN** use full-bleed or near-full-bleed photography as the primary element **BECAUSE** images trigger emotional processing before rational processing.

**IF** the content is explanatory and requires the audience to understand something **THEN** use a dark or white background panel without photography **BECAUSE** photos compete for attention and dilute comprehension of complex structures.

**IF** content has more than two hierarchical levels of complexity **THEN** use a concentric circle or radial diagram **BECAUSE** spatial nesting communicates containment and hierarchy without requiring the audience to read an outline.

**IF** a key assertion could be doubted **THEN** follow it immediately with third-party source validation (press quotes or testimonials) **BECAUSE** external voices carry authority that brand-voice cannot self-grant.

**IF** you use third-party validation **THEN** label the source in the accent color (not the quote) **BECAUSE** red draws the eye to the credibility signal before the content, priming the audience to receive it as authoritative.

**IF** the audience needs to understand the whole framework **THEN** introduce it as a complete diagram first, then revisit the same diagram with individual segments highlighted **BECAUSE** establishing the whole before the parts creates a mental scaffold that makes each part land with more meaning.

**IF** content must be delivered as bullet points **THEN** pair it with a dramatic photo on the opposite half of the slide **BECAUSE** pure text slides read as documents; the photo signals that this information is alive and experiential.

**IF** showing evolution or change over time **THEN** use a horizontal timeline with circular-cropped photography at each node **BECAUSE** circular crops unify disparate images and the horizontal axis reads intuitively as time progression.

**IF** a concept needs to be positioned as a lived-behavior proof (not a claim) **THEN** deliver it as a photo-with-callout-bubble: image shows the behavior, callout names the principle, body text explains the visual connection **BECAUSE** the image is the evidence and the text is the interpretation, not the other way around.

**IF** you need to open a new major section **THEN** use the framework diagram with that section's node highlighted rather than a title slide **BECAUSE** it orients the audience within the whole before diving into the part.

**IF** content is a customer voice (testimonial) **THEN** render it exactly as a review interface would: headline, body, name — no paraphrase **BECAUSE** verbatim quotation is credibility; summary is brand-speak.

**IF** a slide would exceed 80 words of body text **THEN** split it or cut it **BECAUSE** density signals that the creator didn't make hard choices, which undermines confidence in their judgment.

**IF** the deck moves between emotional and analytical modes **THEN** signal the switch with a background color change (dark ↔ white) **BECAUSE** the color shift is a subconscious mode-switch signal that primes the audience for a different type of processing.

**IF** two things must be contrasted **THEN** use equal column widths **BECAUSE** any width imbalance implies one is more important, which defeats the purpose of contrast.

**IF** using icons **THEN** they must be circular, filled with the accent color, and contain only white line-art **BECAUSE** the circle creates consistent visual weight regardless of icon complexity, and the single fill color prevents icon sprawl.

**IF** a claim is abstract **THEN** it needs either a diagram (if structural) or a quote (if emotional) on the same slide — never left as text only **BECAUSE** abstract claims without evidence or structure read as opinions.

**IF** the deck's emotional arc requires intensity **THEN** increase the proportion of dark-background slides and decrease whitespace in those sections **BECAUSE** density and darkness register as seriousness and commitment.

**IF** you are closing a section **THEN** return to the framework diagram (with that section's node highlighted) before moving forward **BECAUSE** bookmarking the framework keeps the audience anchored in the larger structure.

## Sequencing Architecture

**Opening formula:**
Full-bleed identity image → no text hierarchy, logo only → forces the audience to receive the brand viscerally before any claim is made.

**Section formula:**
Framework diagram (whole visible, target segment highlighted) → analytical framework slides (white background, data/structure) → emotional proof slides (dark background, photography) → third-party validation (press or testimonials) → return to framework diagram.

**Transition formula:**
The same diagram repeated with different segments highlighted. The repetition is intentional — it's a map that the audience is navigating, not decoration.

**Closing formula:**
Return to full-bleed photography. The deck closes on feeling, not analysis, because the last frame is the lingering impression.

**Pacing rule:**
No more than three consecutive dark-background slides without a white-background analytical break, and vice versa. The alternation prevents visual fatigue and signals the shift between "here is the evidence" and "here is what it means."

## Anti-Patterns

**Never use gradient backgrounds.** The system is absolute: dark or white. Gradients signal indecision.

**Never use more than one typeface.** All variation is weight, case, and color within a single sans-serif family.

**Never use rectangular image crops.** Photography is either full-bleed (infinite canvas) or circular (contained proof point). Square or landscape crops appear in this system zero times.

**Never use more than one accent color.** `#F44060` is the only emphasis color. Secondary blues, greens, or oranges dilute the signal.

**Never put a chart or graph on a photo background.** Data lives on white or dark solid panels only — never floating over imagery.

**Never use bullet points on a photo slide.** When photography is the primary element, text is the caption, not the content.

**Never use a title slide for section transitions.** The framework diagram performs this function. Title-only slides imply the presenter hasn't earned the transition.

**Never use justified text alignment.** Everything is left-aligned or centered — justified text implies a document, not a presentation.

**Never use decorative borders, rules, or dividers between columns.** Spatial separation alone creates structure. Visible grid lines signal low trust in the layout.

**Never use a font smaller than ~10pt for any label.** If the information can't be read from the back of the room, it's documentation, not a slide.

**Never use stock-photo aesthetics** (white backgrounds, perfect lighting, models looking at camera). Photography must feel observational — caught-in-the-act, dramatic available light, authentic context.
